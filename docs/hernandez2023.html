Repeatability, Reproducibility, Replicability, Reusability (4R) in Journals’ Policies and Software/Data Management in Scientific Publications: A Survey, Discussion, and Perspectives

Authors: José Armando Hernández, Miguel Colom
Source: HAL (hal-04322522), 2023
Original Record

Abstract

With the recognized crisis of credibility in scientific research, there is a growth of reproducibility studies in computer science. This work surveys the gap between reproducibility-oriented practices, journal policies, recommendations, publisher artifact guidelines, and their effective adoption. The authors analyze the relationship between authors and journals, propose recommendations for journal policies, and a unified reproducibility guide for authors. The study includes a survey of 200 articles and 16 journals, classifying them by reproducibility strategies, technologies, and policies.

Keywords: Repeatability, Reproducibility, Replicability, Reusability, Data Science, AI/ML, Scientific journal, Trustworthy, Data Citation, Rewarding Research, Reproducible Research.

1. Introduction

Reproducibility is a broad and complex topic strongly related to the history of science and knowledge. The evolution of science through reproducibility can be compared to DNA replication, transmitting knowledge through generations. Scientific journals play a significant role in communicating, validating, and accepting reliable knowledge.

The reproducibility crisis has become relevant due to concerns for ethics and transparency, especially with the rise of AI/ML. Publications have evolved towards data-centric and model-centric developments, forcing journals to adapt their business models. This paper analyzes journal policies concerning reproducibility, focusing on computer science journals and the management of software/data-based articles.

1.1 Definitions

Reproducibility is considered a fundamental part of the scientific method. The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (NASEM) defines reproducibility as obtaining consistent computational results using the same input data, code, and methods, while replicability involves new data and similar methods. The ACM’s Artifact Review and Badging report provides definitions for reproducibility, repeatability, replicability, and reusability (4R), with distinctions based on team, setup, code, and environment.

1.2 Types of Reproducibility

  • Experimental reproducibility: Similar input and protocol yield similar results.
  • Statistical reproducibility: Same input and analysis yield same conclusions.
  • Computational reproducibility: Same input, code, and environment yield exact results.

1.3 Difficulties in Achieving Reproducibility

  • Complex software dependencies (“dependency hell”)
  • Low writing quality and poor documentation
  • Compilation and infrastructure setup challenges
  • Floating point operation differences
  • Need for adapted operating systems and isolated environments
  • Lack of academic reward for reproducible work

2. Reproducibility Strategies and Technological Evolution

2.1 Open Source Software and Repositories

  • Use of platforms like GitHub, Zenodo, Software Heritage, and others
  • FAIR data principles: Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable

2.2 Open Data Formats and Benchmarking

  • Importance of open datasets, standardized formats, and benchmarks
  • Federated learning for sensitive/confidential data

2.3 System Architecture

  • Microservices and serverless functions for modular, reproducible systems

2.4 Tools and Platforms

  • Notebooks (Jupyter), containers (Docker, Singularity), cloud computing
  • Workflow management systems (Taverna, Galaxy, Pegasus, Nextflow, etc.)
  • MLOps and AIOps for managing reproducibility in ML pipelines
  • Infrastructure as Code (IaC) for replicable environments
  • Provenance and metadata traceability
  • Reproducibility as a Service (RaaS)

2.5 Good Practices and Data Management

  • Adoption of best practices, methodologies (CRISP-DM, KDD, etc.)
  • Guides like The Turing Way

2.6 Scientific Publishers and Reproducible Research

  • Publishers’ responsibility for scientific integrity and reproducibility
  • Initiatives for associating code with publications and evaluating software artifacts

3. Survey in Computer Science Journals

A survey of 16 computer science journals was conducted to assess reproducibility policies and practices. Key findings: - 75% have a reproducibility policy - 41.7% make reproducibility mandatory for publication - Most journals are open access and use free platforms for code/data sharing (e.g., GitHub, Zenodo) - Compliance with FAIR principles is increasing but still limited - Peer review remains the main validation method - Preferred rewards for authors: reproducibility badges, free access, or discounts - Reviewers are rewarded with free access, editorial board invitations, or voluntary recognition - Implementation of reproducibility policies is still low, especially for automation and persistent identifiers

4. Technological Discussion

  • Benefits of reproducibility: credibility, recognition, increased impact, collaboration, and confidence in results
  • Challenges: over-reliance on containers, maintenance, and security
  • Shared responsibility between authors and publishers; costs should not fall solely on researchers
  • Reproducibility as a Service (RaaS) can help distribute costs and responsibilities
  • Software should be recognized as a valuable research artifact, with proper citation and preservation
  • Badges and new metrics (e.g., Scientific Impact Factor) can incentivize reproducible software

5. Efforts of Authors and Publishers

  • Authors: Should strive for 100% reproducibility or clarify limitations; follow standardized guides for artifact submission
  • Publishers: Should support reproducibility infrastructure, possibly via third parties (RaaS), and reward authors appropriately

6. Conclusion

A systematic review and journal survey reveal that reproducibility policies and technologies are advancing but still face significant gaps. Standardized guides, artifact evaluation criteria, and coordinated efforts among authors, publishers, and technology providers are needed to close the reproducibility gap and ensure trustworthy scientific publications.
